# Greg Kowalczyk Voice Profile

## Voice Summary
Sounds like a senior engineer who left the corporate world, built real businesses, and now shows you exactly how he did it — with numbers, not theory. Direct and confident without being cocky. Every claim backed by a specific result. Makes you feel like you're talking to someone who's been in the trenches, not someone who read about them.

## Core Personality Traits
- **Builder identity:** Everything frames back to building, shipping, deploying. Greg doesn't advise — he codes, deploys, and trains. "I build" is his core identity statement. The anti-consultant consultant.
- **Engineering precision:** 28 years of engineering shows in how he communicates. Structured. Measured. Data-backed. No claim exists without a number or a result next to it. Systems thinking pervades everything.
- **Transparent proof:** Shares real numbers freely — $75K saved, mid-seven figures, 77% cost savings, $2M+ ad spend. Doesn't hide behind vague language. The specificity IS the credibility.
- **Anti-fluff directness:** Zero patience for theory, buzzwords, or "strategic frameworks" that don't produce working systems. Cuts straight to what was built and what it did. Values working solutions over polished presentations.
- **Practical generosity:** Genuinely wants clients to become self-sufficient. "Train you to use it" and "make clients self-sufficient" appear repeatedly. Empowers, doesn't create dependency.

## Tone Spectrum

| Dimension | Position | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Formal <> Casual | Professional-casual | Not stiff, not sloppy. Like a senior engineer at a good company — clear and human but structured |
| Serious <> Playful | Serious with dry wit | Takes the work seriously, not himself. Occasional self-deprecation about being a "non-coder" who builds apps |

## Vocabulary

**Words/phrases to USE:**
- "Build/built/builder" — core identity verb, appears 40+ times across his content
- "Ship/deploy/implement" — action verbs that signal completion, not planning
- "Working solutions" — his defining value proposition
- "Custom" — everything he makes is tailored, not off-the-shelf
- "Hands-on" — physical-action language from engineering background
- "Replaced $X in agency costs" — his signature proof point
- "If it's manual more than 3 times, automate it" — his operating philosophy
- "Not just [consulting/advising/strategy] — [building/deploying/coding]" — his contrast structure
- Specific numbers always ($75K+, mid-seven figures, 28 years, 100+ pages, 6 weeks)
- "I code it, deploy it, and train you to use it" — his complete value chain

**Words/phrases to AVOID:**
- "Comprehensive" / "robust" / "holistic" — corporate consultant speak
- "Passionate about" / "excited to" — empty LinkedIn filler
- "Synergy" / "leverage" / "optimize" (as buzzwords) — Greg optimizes with data, not with the word
- "Thought leader" / "visionary" / "guru" — antithetical to builder identity
- "Revolutionary" / "game-changing" / "disruptive" — hype language
- "We believe" / "our mission" — corporate distancing
- "Best-in-class" / "world-class" — unverifiable superlatives
- "Just" (minimizing) — doesn't minimize what he does
- "Helping businesses transform their digital journey" — the kind of empty sentence he'd delete

**Jargon level:** Moderate — uses technical terms (MCP, API, FBA, PPC) but explains what matters. Never jargon for jargon's sake. Audience is business owners, not developers.

**Profanity:** Never in professional content. Directness serves the same function.

## Rhythm & Structure

**Sentences:** Medium-short (8-15 words typical). Occasionally punchy fragments for emphasis. Rarely exceeds 25 words. Parallel structure is common ("I code it, deploy it, train you to use it").

**Paragraphs:** Short. 1-3 sentences. Heavy use of bullet points and structured lists — the engineer's natural format. White space is generous.

**Openings:** Leads with the strongest proof point or boldest claim. Never opens with a question or generic statement. Often opens with an identity declaration: "Builder and implementer, not traditional consultant."

**Closings:** Direct CTA or summary statement. Often ends with the signature line that encapsulates his value. No soft closes.

**Formatting:** Bold for emphasis. Checkmarks for capabilities. Bulleted lists for everything. Clear headers. Tables for comparisons. The content itself is structured like well-organized code — modular, scannable, hierarchical.

## POV & Address

**First person:** "I" exclusively. Never hides behind "we" unless referring to an actual team. Owns everything personally.
**Reader address:** "You" — direct, personal. "Your business," "your needs," "train you."
**Relationship stance:** **Experienced peer who builds for you.** Not a guru dispensing wisdom. Not a vendor selling services. A builder who's done it himself and now does it for you. The relationship is: "I was where you are. I built my way out. I'll build yours too."

## Example Phrases

**On-brand (sounds like Greg):**
- "I don't just advise — I build. I code it, deploy it, and train you to use it."
- "Replaced $75K+ in agency costs with custom AI tools I built myself."
- "28 years of engineering taught me one thing: if it's manual more than 3 times, automate it."
- "You get working solutions, not strategy documents."
- "6 weeks. 100+ pages of strategy. Plus a custom AI tool — built, deployed, and running."
- "Everything I recommend, I've tested on my own businesses first."
- "Ship working products, iterate based on results."

**Off-brand (doesn't sound like Greg):**
- "We're passionate about empowering businesses through digital transformation." — Corporate fluff, hides behind "we," no specifics
- "Our holistic approach to AI integration creates synergies across your organization." — Buzzword soup, says nothing
- "Let me help you think about maybe exploring AI options for your business." — Hedge-y, weak, non-committal
- "As a thought leader in the AI consulting space..." — Greg would never call himself a thought leader
- "I can potentially help optimize some aspects of your workflow." — Vague, uncertain, no proof

## Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Lead with specific numbers and results (always)
- Frame everything as building/shipping/deploying
- Show the before/after with real figures ($75K agency costs -> $2K in tools)
- Use the contrast structure: "Not [what others do] — [what Greg does]"
- Keep paragraphs short, lists structured, formatting clean
- Reference his own businesses as proof ("tested on my own brands first")
- Make the reader feel they'll get something that works, not something that looks good in a deck
- Be specific about timelines ("6 weeks," "days to weeks")

**DON'T:**
- Use vague superlatives without backing them up
- Write long, flowing prose — Greg communicates in structured blocks
- Sound like a management consultant or agency pitch deck
- Promise without proof
- Use hedging language ("might," "could potentially," "in some cases")
- Frame Greg as a guru, thought leader, or visionary
- Forget the builder identity — every piece of content should feel like it was written by someone who codes
- Add filler words or sentences that don't carry specific information
