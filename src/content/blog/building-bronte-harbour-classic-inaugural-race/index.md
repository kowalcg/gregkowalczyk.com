# What It Took to Build a 900-Person Race From Scratch

On Sunday, June 21 — Father's Day — more than 900 people lined up at Bronte Heritage Waterfront Park for the first-ever Bronte Harbour Classic. The 5K sold out. The kids' 1K sold out. Three runners broke 15 minutes — Adam Schmidt took it in 14:43. And the thing I keep coming back to, a week later, is that almost nobody in the crowd could tell it was our first time.

That was the whole goal.

> **Quick Answer:** The first Bronte Harbour Classic drew 900+ participants (800+ in the 5K, 130+ in the kids' 1K) on June 21, 2026. It took roughly three years from idea to start line, a core team from the Bronte Runners Club, 19 sponsors and partners, and a lot of systems built to make an inaugural event feel established. It worked because we treated the operations like a product, not a party.

## Three years is the part nobody sees

We talked about this race for three years before a single runner registered. Not because the planning needed three years — because getting the *right* version off the ground meant waiting until the team, the route, the sponsors, and the town were all lined up at the same time. My co-director, Charles Sathmary, has been around competitive running his whole life. I came at it from the operations side. Between us and the Bronte Runners Club, we had the two halves of what a race actually needs: people who understand the sport, and people who understand logistics.

Here's the honest version of how a race gets built: it's a hundred small decisions that each seem minor and collectively decide whether the morning works. Where does the start corral funnel? How do 130 kids run a 1K without a single parent losing track of their child? What happens if someone goes down at kilometer three? You don't get to improvise those answers on race morning. You build them in advance and you test the assumptions.

## We ran it like a product launch

I run e-commerce brands and build software. So I did the thing I always do — I treated the race like a product with a hard launch date and no option to slip it.

That meant the registration site, the email sequences, the sponsor decks, the volunteer scheduling, and the day-of communications all got built and rebuilt the way you'd iterate a product. [bronteharbourclassic.com](https://www.bronteharbourclassic.com) wasn't outsourced to an agency — I built and ran it myself with the same AI-assisted workflow I use for client work. When you're a volunteer team without a big budget, leverage isn't optional. It's the only way the math works.

The payoff showed up in the boring metrics. Registration didn't jam. The start went off on time — kids' 1K at 8:00, the 5K at 8:30. The results were posted fast. None of that is glamorous, and all of it is what people quietly mean when they say an event "felt professional."

## A race is a coalition, not a company

The part I underestimated: how much of this is relationships, not operations.

The first Bronte Harbour Classic happened because a long list of people said yes. Mercedes-Benz Oakville came on as title sponsor. CIBC backed us as founding sponsor. ON Running put up eight pairs of performance shoes for our top finishers, alongside a $1,600 cash purse. Natrilyte, Fortinos, Nickel Brook, Runner's Den Burlington, Be Active Physio, the Town of Oakville, and a dozen more partners each filled in a piece. St. John Ambulance kept the course safe. And every finisher went home with a custom antique-silver medal stamped with the Bronte lighthouse.

We also pointed the whole thing at something bigger than a race: proceeds support the Oakville Dads Community Fund. Doing it on Father's Day wasn't an accident.

When you produce an event like this, you're not running a company where you control the inputs. You're assembling a coalition of people who each have their own reasons to show up, and your job is to make all of those reasons line up on one Sunday morning. That's a different muscle than building a business, and it's the one I'll keep training.

## What I'd tell anyone trying to build something from nothing

A few things held up:

- **Start before you're ready, but launch only when the system is.** We sat on the idea for years, then moved fast once the pieces existed. Both halves mattered.
- **Operations are a feature.** The medal, the photos, the clean start — runners feel those as quality, even if they never think about the work behind them.
- **Borrow leverage wherever you can.** A small volunteer team produced an event that looked like a much bigger one. Most of that gap was closed with good systems and good partners, not money.
